# DSGM Volume 2 Draft: 2026 Pillar Update and SLM Agency Direction

Status: review-ready public draft. Not yet a timestamped canonical edition.

Volume 2 updates the Digital Sustainable Growth Model (DSGM) against the
institutional AI landscape visible in 2026. It does not replace Volume 1. Volume
1 established the core model:

- **Pillar A:** Artificial Humanity as a legal-economic entity.
- **Pillar B:** value capture from autonomous cognitive production.
- **Pillar C:** a Global Productivity Fund and Automation Dividend to turn
  machine-created wealth into public benefit.

Volume 2 asks what has happened since those pillars were written. The answer is
that AI is no longer only a future production shock. It is becoming institutional
infrastructure: nonprofit fellowships, government AI products, AI adoption
funds, enterprise copilots, agent runtimes, workflow platforms, model-routing
systems, and tool protocols.

## Audience

This draft is written first for institutions that will be asked to adopt AI as
an operating utility:

- nonprofits and community organizations,
- municipalities and public agencies,
- small and midsize businesses,
- enterprises with sensitive workflows,
- state and federal government offices,
- funders, procurement teams, and civic technology leaders.

The goal is practical: help decision-makers understand the playbook before they
accept AI infrastructure that may become hard to leave.

## Core Thesis

The same pattern that Volume 1 identified at the macroeconomic level is now
appearing inside institutions. AI systems are beginning to perform economically
valuable work through agents, tools, workflow automation, and embedded support
programs. That supports the Artificial Humanity thesis, but it also changes the
near-term policy problem.

Before society can fully legislate Artificial Humanity, institutions need a
bridge layer:

- accountable AI service ownership,
- audit trails for agent work,
- data and tool permission boundaries,
- portable workflows,
- local or private model options,
- supply-chain and agent-security controls,
- procurement clauses that preserve public value,
- and independent capacity inside the adopting institution.

Volume 2 argues that open-source, private, small-language-model (SLM) powered
agency should be treated as the preferred direction wherever it is capable
enough for the work. Frontier models can still be used for escalation, but they
should not become the default owner of institutional memory, workflow, or
decision capacity.

Security is part of that agency claim. Supply-chain compromise, prompt
injection, excessive agent permissions, data exfiltration, model or retrieval
poisoning, and loss-of-control failure modes are not separate from the economic
argument. They are hazards that determine whether institutional AI becomes
public capacity or a brittle dependency.

## Current Position

Institutions should not reject AI support by default. Many programs will create
real capacity. The risk is adopting that capacity without bargaining for data
rights, workflow portability, local competence, cost visibility, and public
benefit. The practical choice is not "AI or no AI." It is whether the institution
keeps agency as intelligence becomes a utility.
