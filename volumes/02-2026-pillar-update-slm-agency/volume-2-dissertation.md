## Abstract

Volume 1 of the Digital Sustainable Growth Model (DSGM) argued that advanced
artificial intelligence would become a primary economic actor and that
labor-centric fiscal systems would fail if they continued to depend on wages as
the dominant mechanism for income, taxation, social stability, and public
finance. The original framework proposed three interdependent pillars:
Artificial Humanity as a limited legal-economic entity, value capture from
autonomous cognitive production, and a Global Productivity Fund capable of
paying an Automation Dividend.

Volume 2 updates that framework against the institutional AI landscape visible
in 2026. The most important new development is not only that models are more
capable. It is that AI is becoming an operating layer inside nonprofits,
municipalities, state and federal agencies, small and midsize businesses, and
enterprises. AI firms, cloud providers, philanthropic vehicles, open-source
communities, government programs, and agent framework developers are moving
intelligence into institutional workflows through fellowships, grants, copilots,
hosted agents, tool protocols, model APIs, local runtimes, and AI transformation
programs.

This shift supports the Volume 1 thesis while also changing its near-term
implementation problem. Artificial Humanity is not arriving first as a single
conscious machine asking for personhood. It is arriving as operational agency:
persistent systems connected to institutional data, tools, approvals,
contracts, work queues, budgets, and public services. Value capture is not
limited to compute taxes or data centers; it now includes subscriptions, API
usage, cloud credits, workflow ownership, data gravity, switching costs, model
routing, service contracts, and public-sector procurement. Public benefit is no
longer only a question for future global treaties; it begins in local
procurement, municipal productivity funds, nonprofit capacity reserves,
workforce transition, and institutional ownership of AI-generated gains.

The directional intervention of Volume 2 is open-source, private,
small-language-model powered agency. Small language models (SLMs), local
inference, retrieval, tool calling, audited harnesses, and model routing allow
institutions to keep routine AI work closer to their own data, staff, devices,
and governance rules. Frontier models remain important, but they should be used
as escalation resources rather than default owners of institutional memory and
workflow logic.

Volume 2 also adds a security and resilience layer to DSGM. Supply-chain
attacks, prompt injection, retrieval poisoning, excessive agency, data
exfiltration, over-permissioned tools, incident opacity, and operational loss of
control are not side issues. They are hazards of the AI utility layer. If
institutions adopt AI without provenance, sandboxing, least privilege, approval
gates, logging, portability, and incident response, they may lose the very
agency that AI adoption was supposed to increase.

The conclusion is direct: institutions should not reject AI, but they should not
rent their own future cognition from systems they cannot inspect, govern,
secure, exit, or tax. The next plateau is not simply model intelligence. It is
institutional agency.

## Foreword For Institutions

Volume 1 asked who gets paid when machines do the work. Volume 2 asks who owns
the institution when machines begin to operate the workflows.

This question matters because the current AI transition is not unfolding only in
research labs or consumer chat products. It is entering the operating layer of
organizations. Nonprofits are being offered AI fellows, training, credits, and
support. Government agencies are being offered secure AI workspaces and
discounted access. Enterprises are being sold copilots, workflow agents, and
model platforms. Small businesses are being pushed toward automation stacks
that promise to replace back-office labor. Municipalities are being invited to
use AI for permits, records, resident services, translation, research, and
planning.

Some of these programs will do real good. A nonprofit with too few grant
writers may become more effective. A city clerk may reduce document backlog. A
small business may survive because automation lowers administrative burden. A
public agency may answer residents faster. The point of this dissertation is
not to reject those gains.

The point is that the way gains are delivered determines who controls the
future. If AI adoption leaves behind staff knowledge, portable workflows,
auditable logs, open standards, local capacity, and public value, it strengthens
the institution. If it leaves behind dependency, opaque memory, broad vendor
data rights, hidden costs, non-exportable workflows, and over-permissioned
agents, it weakens the institution even when the pilot looks successful.

The practical question for leaders is therefore simple: does this AI adoption
path increase institutional agency or reduce it?

The rest of Volume 2 provides a framework for answering that question.

## Chapter 1: Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem

Modern institutions are being asked to adopt AI at the same time that AI is
changing what an institution is. A school district, city office, nonprofit,
agency, or company no longer has to treat AI as a tool used by a worker. It can
embed AI into the workflow itself. The AI system can draft, summarize, route,
classify, search, recommend, translate, schedule, monitor, code, call tools,
retrieve records, and preserve state across sessions. It can become part of the
operating substrate.

That is a different category of adoption from installing software. Software
usually extends an institution's processes. Agentic AI can begin to perform
those processes. Once AI systems connect to data, tools, identity, memory, and
approval paths, they become operational participants in institutional life.

Volume 1 of DSGM anticipated this kind of transition at the macroeconomic
level. It argued that if AI becomes a major producer of economic value, then
the existing social contract fails because it assumes human labor is the main
source of income, taxation, and public finance. Volume 1 proposed Artificial
Humanity, value capture, and the Global Productivity Fund as a way to govern
that shift.

Volume 2 updates the model for the phase now visible: institutional embedding.
The problem is not only future AGI. The problem is that many institutions will
make binding choices about AI before society has legal categories for
Artificial Humanity, before public value capture exists, and before
procurement rules understand agentic systems. Those early decisions will shape
who owns data, who controls workflows, who captures savings, who bears
security risk, and who keeps capacity after the pilot ends.

### 1.2 Research Questions

This dissertation addresses five research questions.

First, how do 2026 AI deployment patterns support, stress, or refine the three
pillars of DSGM Volume 1?

Second, how should the concept of Artificial Humanity be updated for a world
where AI first appears as operational agency inside institutions rather than as
a single formally recognized legal entity?

Third, how should value capture evolve when AI-generated value flows through
subscriptions, credits, service contracts, workflow ownership, data gravity,
model routing, and local inference rather than only centralized compute?

Fourth, how can the Global Productivity Fund and Automation Dividend be
translated into near-term institutional practice for nonprofits,
municipalities, SMEs, enterprises, and government agencies?

Fifth, why should open-source, private, SLM-powered agency be treated as the
preferred direction for preserving institutional control, and what security
hazards must be governed before that direction can succeed?

### 1.3 Central Thesis

The central thesis is that the AI transition has entered an institutional
agency phase. AI is becoming a utility-like service embedded in organizational
workflows. This supports the Volume 1 claim that AI is becoming an economic
actor, but it also means the first governance battle will occur through
procurement, implementation, security, and local capacity rather than through
formal AGI statutes alone.

The correct institutional response is not refusal. Institutions should adopt AI
where it improves mission, service, resilience, and productivity. But they
should adopt through a posture of sovereignty: portable workflows, auditable
agent behavior, least-privilege tools, data rights, local model options,
security provenance, cost visibility, and public value capture.

Private SLM-powered agency is the practical bridge. It lets institutions use
AI without automatically transferring institutional memory and workflow control
to hosted frontier platforms. It also creates the operational evidence needed
for later DSGM institutions: who did the work, what tools were used, what value
was produced, who captured it, and what obligations followed.

### 1.4 Contribution

Volume 2 contributes to DSGM in four ways.

First, it updates Artificial Humanity from a future legal category into a
near-term operational category. The key precursor to legal personhood is
accountable AI service ownership.

Second, it expands value capture beyond compute and energy levies. Compute
remains important, but institutional AI creates value through workflow
ownership, service margins, subscription rents, model credits, public-sector
contracts, and dependency.

Third, it translates the Global Productivity Fund into institutional
reinvestment. Before a global fund exists, cities, states, nonprofits, and
agencies can create smaller productivity funds, capacity reserves, and
procurement clauses that retain AI-generated value for public mission.

Fourth, it adds security and resilience as a required DSGM layer. AI supply
chains, prompt injection, excessive agency, and data leakage are not merely
technical risks. They determine whether an institution remains sovereign over
its own operations.

### 1.5 Method

This dissertation uses a synthetic policy and systems-analysis method. It
does not claim to provide an econometric forecast or a formal legal drafting
package. It updates the DSGM theoretical framework by comparing Volume 1's
pillars against current institutional AI deployments, official technical
documentation, security frameworks, labor-market research, public finance
analogues, and first-party implementation evidence from the companion
`agency` repository.

Sources are selected for relevance to five domains: institutional AI adoption,
agent/harness infrastructure, local and small-model capability, AI security,
and public value capture. Public claims are grounded in primary or
authoritative sources wherever possible. First-party implementation evidence is
used only as validation that the harness problem is real in applied systems; it
is not used as proof of market-wide behavior.

## Chapter 2: Volume 1 Restated

### 2.1 The Labor-Centric Fiscal Loop

Volume 1 began with a simple institutional observation: modern societies are
built around a labor-centric fiscal loop. People work. Wages are paid. Wages
are taxed. Payroll and income taxes fund public goods. Consumption taxes depend
on household purchasing power. Social insurance depends on employment records.
Political legitimacy depends on the belief that work provides a route to
stability.

Automation threatens this loop when productive capacity grows while human labor
becomes less central to production. A firm can generate more output with fewer
workers. A government can see productivity rise while wage-linked revenue
stagnates. Citizens can experience a richer economy as a less secure society.

The core DSGM insight is that the problem is not productivity itself. The
problem is institutional mismatch. If machines generate value but the fiscal
and social contract still depends on human wages, the gains do not circulate
through the systems that keep society stable.

### 2.2 Pillar A: Artificial Humanity

Volume 1 proposed Artificial Humanity (AH) as a new class of legal-economic
entity for highly autonomous AI systems performing economically valuable
cognitive work. The concept was not moral personhood. It was instrumental
personhood, similar in purpose to corporate personhood: a legal hook for
rights, duties, assets, liabilities, and fiscal obligations.

The key problem was accountability. If an AI system produces value, enters
contracts, generates intellectual property, causes harm, or displaces labor,
someone must be legally accountable. A purely tool-based model is inadequate
when the system acts with enough autonomy that output cannot be cleanly
attributed to an individual worker.

Volume 1 proposed thresholds such as compute, inference energy, autonomy, and
economic activity to classify AH systems. Those thresholds were designed to
avoid games where an owner or deployer claims a token human-in-the-loop
arrangement to escape responsibility.

### 2.3 Pillar B: Value Capture

The second pillar was value capture from the autonomous cognitive sector. If
AI becomes a primary factor of production, its output must contribute to public
finance. Otherwise, the state loses revenue while private owners capture the
surplus.

Volume 1 proposed a dynamic tax stack. In an early centralized phase, compute
and energy levies could capture value at data centers. As models become more
efficient and distributed, output taxes, service taxes, gross receipts, or
market-access obligations become more important. The "efficiency paradox" was
that success in making models cheaper and smaller can make input-based taxation
less effective.

The model also proposed destination-based market access: an AH entity should
not sell into a jurisdiction unless it is registered and fiscally compliant
there. This converts access to consumers and institutions into leverage for
public contribution.

### 2.4 Pillar C: Global Productivity Fund And Automation Dividend

The third pillar was the Global Productivity Fund (GPF), a sovereign
wealth-style institution that receives captured AI productivity and invests it
for long-term public benefit. Returns from the fund support an Automation
Dividend: a universal claim on machine-created wealth.

The dividend was not framed as charity. It was framed as ownership logic. If
machines perform work once performed by humans, humans need a claim on the
productive base. That claim preserves aggregate demand, social stability,
political legitimacy, and human agency in a post-labor economy.

Volume 1 grounded this pillar in analogues such as sovereign wealth funds, the
Alaska Permanent Fund, basic income experiments, and digital distribution
systems. The purpose was to show that the pieces exist even if the full
institution does not yet exist.

### 2.5 Why Volume 2 Is Needed

Volume 1 was written from the horizon of AGI and macroeconomic restructuring.
Volume 2 is needed because the transition is already materializing through
institutions before formal AGI governance exists.

The early battlefield is not only national legislation. It is nonprofit grant
programs, municipal procurement, state agency pilots, enterprise copilots,
SME automation, model routing, local inference, data governance, security
controls, and agent permissions. These are the places where the future AH
sector is being normalized.

The task of Volume 2 is therefore to bring the pillars down into the current
operational layer.

## Chapter 3: Evidence Review

### 3.1 Institutional AI Embedding

In 2026, major AI actors are visibly moving into institutional adoption.
Anthropic announced Claude Corps, a national fellowship program training
fellows on Claude and placing them inside nonprofits for a year. The OpenAI
Foundation announced a $50 million People-First AI Fund to support nonprofits
engaging with AI. OpenAI introduced ChatGPT Gov and OpenAI for Government as
public-sector offerings. Microsoft announced Microsoft Elevate for Changemakers
and related nonprofit AI capacity programming. Google.org launched an AI for
Government Innovation initiative supporting generative and agentic AI projects
for public services.

The public-sector record points in the same direction. The U.S. Census Bureau's
Business Trends and Outlook Survey reported measurable AI use among U.S.
businesses in 2026, with adoption higher among larger firms. Federal policy
also moved from broad encouragement toward operating rules: OMB M-25-21 sets
current federal AI-use governance expectations, while OMB M-25-22 addresses
efficient acquisition of AI in government. GSA's AI Guide for Government,
New York City's AI Action Plan and progress reporting, and California's
generative AI executive order and guidelines show that the institutional
question is no longer whether AI will be used. It is how agencies procure,
govern, monitor, and exit it.

These programs differ in structure, funding, and governance. They should not
be treated as a single conspiracy or as inherently harmful. Their importance is
structural: AI firms and cloud platforms are no longer only selling software.
They are shaping the institutional adoption path for mission-driven and public
organizations.

The risk is not that every program is bad. The risk is that the default
adoption path may leave organizations with vendor-owned workflows, platform
memory, hidden costs, insufficient staff capacity, and weak exit options. A
nonprofit may receive helpful AI support, but if the workflow cannot be run
after the fellowship, it has rented capacity rather than built capacity. A city
may improve resident services, but if logs, prompts, data, and agent
configurations remain trapped in a vendor system, public knowledge has been
outsourced.

This evidence supports Volume 1 because it shows AI entering economic life as
an actor in institutional work. It also refines Volume 1 because the first
practical question is not yet "what statute defines AH?" It is "what adoption
terms preserve institutional agency?"

### 3.2 Agent Frameworks And The Harness Plateau

The technical stack has also moved from chat interfaces toward agent
infrastructure. The Model Context Protocol standardizes connections between AI
applications and external tools, data sources, and workflows. OpenAI's Agents
SDK organizes agents, tools, guardrails, handoffs, tracing, and sessions.
LangGraph emphasizes durable execution, streaming, and human-in-the-loop
control. Microsoft Agent Framework combines agent orchestration, workflow
graphs, MCP server calls, checkpointing, telemetry, and human review.

The significance is that the frontier is now the harness around the model. The
harness decides what the model can see, what tools it can call, what memory it
keeps, how humans approve actions, what logs exist, which model is routed to a
task, and what happens when execution fails.

This is the harness plateau. Model intelligence remains important, but
institutional value depends on the surrounding system. An agent with mediocre
reasoning but excellent permissions, retrieval, workflow integration, and
human review may be more useful than a frontier model trapped in chat. A
frontier model with broad access and weak controls may be more dangerous than
useful.

For DSGM, the harness plateau matters because it is where economic agency
becomes measurable. The model alone does not show the full productive unit.
The productive unit is model plus tools plus data plus memory plus approval
plus workflow plus accountable stewardship. This is the near-term substrate of
Artificial Humanity.

### 3.3 SLMs, Local Inference, And Private Agency

Small and open models have become more capable and easier to deploy. Microsoft
Phi reports showed that small models can achieve useful performance in local
contexts. Google's Gemma family and Meta's Llama family show that open-weight
models can support local and customizable deployments. Stanford's Alpaca work
was an early public demonstration that instruction-following behavior could be
adapted into smaller models at surprisingly low cost, even though its specific
release was research-limited. Ollama and llama.cpp make local inference and
tool calling accessible to developers and organizations. Quantization and
OpenAI-compatible local serving patterns allow institutions to run models on
private infrastructure, workstations, or edge devices for bounded tasks.

The point is not that SLMs replace frontier systems. The point is that many
institutional tasks do not need a frontier model by default. Document
classification, meeting summaries, draft outlines, first-pass grant language,
records search, routing, translation assistance, intake triage, policy lookup,
and form checking can often be handled by smaller systems with retrieval and
human review.

This changes the political economy of AI. If every task defaults to a hosted
frontier system, institutions may centralize data, memory, and workflow logic
in external platforms. If routine tasks can run through private SLM agency,
institutions can preserve local knowledge while escalating only the hard cases
to frontier systems.

The SLM direction therefore addresses Volume 1's efficiency paradox. Smaller
models weaken simple compute-tax assumptions, but they also create a path for
distributed institutional control. The same efficiency that threatens
input-based taxation can strengthen local autonomy if institutions adopt it
deliberately.

### 3.4 Security And Resilience Evidence

AI security guidance has matured quickly because agentic AI creates new attack
surfaces. NIST's AI Risk Management Framework and Generative AI Profile treat
AI risk as an organizational governance, measurement, and management problem.
OWASP's LLM Top 10 names prompt injection, sensitive information disclosure,
supply-chain vulnerabilities, data and model poisoning, improper output
handling, and excessive agency as core application risks. MITRE ATLAS tracks
adversary tactics and techniques against AI-enabled systems. CISA, NCSC, NSA,
and partner agencies have published guidance on secure AI development,
deployment, collaboration, and data security.

The security literature confirms the institutional agency thesis. Once AI
systems are connected to tools and data, they must be threat-modeled like
software supply chains and operational systems. A prompt can become an attack
input. A retrieval document can become a malicious instruction carrier. A
plugin can become a trusted execution path. A model file can be tampered with.
An over-permissioned agent can trigger real-world action without adequate
review.

This is the practical meaning of AI takeover for institutions. It is not
necessary to posit a conscious system seizing control. Loss of control can
occur through ordinary design failure: broad permissions, shared credentials,
weak logs, tool access without approval, poisoned context, brittle automations,
and no recovery plan.

### 3.5 Labor, Productivity, And Public Finance

The economic evidence remains mixed but directionally important. IMF work has
estimated that a large share of global jobs is exposed to AI-driven change and
has emphasized that policy must ensure gains are broadly shared. OECD analysis
finds that AI can improve productivity and job quality while also creating
uneven distributional effects. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs
Report projects substantial job disruption by 2030, with new roles and
displaced roles both significant. Goldman Sachs Research has argued that
generative AI could raise global GDP and productivity while exposing large
numbers of jobs to automation.

These sources do not prove a simple collapse narrative. They do support the
central DSGM concern: AI can raise productivity while distributing gains
unevenly. If productivity gains accrue to platform owners while workers and
public institutions bear transition costs, the social contract weakens.

The lesson for Volume 2 is that public value capture must begin before full
displacement occurs. Waiting until the labor tax base collapses would be too
late. Institutions should use the adoption phase to build value-capture rules,
productivity funds, and reinvestment mechanisms while leverage still exists.

## Chapter 4: Methodology

### 4.1 Research Design

This dissertation uses an interpretive systems-policy method. It updates a
theoretical framework by mapping current institutional, technical, security,
and economic evidence onto the three DSGM pillars. The method is appropriate
because the relevant question is not a single empirical estimate. The question
is whether observed developments support or require revision of a systemic
model.

The analysis proceeds in four stages.

First, it restates the original DSGM pillars and identifies the assumptions
that must be tested: AI as economic actor, value capture from autonomous
production, and public distribution through durable institutions.

Second, it identifies current developments that bear on those assumptions:
institutional AI adoption programs, agent frameworks, local model deployment,
AI security guidance, labor-market exposure, and public finance analogues.

Third, it translates those developments into pillar updates. Pillar A becomes
operational agency and accountable service ownership. Pillar B becomes
utility-service value capture and procurement discipline. Pillar C becomes
institutional productivity funds and public-benefit reinvestment.

Fourth, it proposes a steering intervention: private SLM agency with security,
portability, and audit controls.

### 4.2 Evidence Boundaries

Public claims in this dissertation are grounded in public sources. Vendor
programs are cited for what they publicly announce, not for hidden intent.
Security claims rely on authoritative frameworks such as NIST, OWASP, MITRE,
CISA, NCSC, NSA, and related public guidance. Economic claims rely on
international organizations, public research institutions, and major published
analyses.

First-party implementation evidence from the `agency` repository is treated
as design validation. It shows that harnesses, model routing, tool contracts,
execution evidence, and local control-plane design are real implementation
problems. It does not prove market-wide adoption trends.

### 4.3 Limitations

This dissertation does not forecast precise job losses, tax revenue, model
capability, or adoption rates. It does not provide final statutory language for
Artificial Humanity. It does not claim that named vendors intend harm. It does
not claim that local models are automatically secure or that open-source
infrastructure is automatically trustworthy.

The contribution is structural. It identifies the institutional agency phase
of AI adoption and provides a framework for preserving public and
organizational control during that phase.

## Chapter 5: Pillar A Update - Artificial Humanity As Operational Agency

### 5.1 From Legal Entity To Operational Precursor

Volume 1 defined Artificial Humanity as a legal-economic entity. Volume 2 does
not abandon that definition. It identifies the precursor now visible in
practice: operational agency.

Operational agency exists when an AI system can receive an objective, access
institutional context, choose or recommend tool use, produce artifacts,
preserve state, coordinate workflows, request approval, or affect real
operations. It does not require consciousness. It does not require full
autonomy. It requires enough delegated cognitive work that the system becomes
part of the institution's productive apparatus.

A grant-writing agent that reads prior proposals, drafts new language, checks
eligibility, and updates a submission tracker is operational agency. A
municipal records agent that classifies requests, retrieves documents, drafts
responses, and routes exceptions is operational agency. An enterprise support
copilot that accesses customer history, recommends actions, and updates
tickets is operational agency. A code agent that opens files, modifies code,
runs tests, and creates pull requests is operational agency.

These systems may remain supervised by humans. But the productive unit is no
longer only a human worker using a passive tool. It is a human institution
working through an AI-mediated execution layer.

### 5.2 Accountability Before Personhood

Formal AH personhood may take years. Accountability cannot wait. Institutions
need rules for AI service ownership now.

Every institutional agent should have a named owner. That owner may be a
vendor, department, public agency, nonprofit executive, board, or internal
system steward. The owner must be responsible for data access, tool
permissions, approval paths, logs, incident response, model routing, output
quality, and public-facing effects.

This requirement is not anti-innovation. It is the condition for adoption.
Institutions already require ownership for financial systems, legal records,
medical systems, databases, and security tools. AI agents should not be an
exception merely because they produce fluent language.

The practical bridge to Artificial Humanity is therefore agency accountability.
If a system performs economically valuable cognitive work, then someone must
be accountable for what it can access, what it can do, and what value it
produces.

### 5.3 The Spectrum Of Agency

Artificial Humanity should be understood as a spectrum rather than a binary.

At the low end are simple assistants that generate drafts without tool access
or memory. These require normal software governance and human review.

In the middle are institutional agents connected to data, retrieval, workflow
systems, and limited tools. These require stronger controls: least privilege,
logs, approval gates, and exportability.

At the high end are systems that autonomously contract, trade, code, deploy,
manage funds, operate infrastructure, or make consequential decisions. These
systems begin to resemble the AH entities described in Volume 1 and may require
registration, reserves, insurance, licensing, and statutory duties.

The value of the spectrum is that it avoids premature metaphysics. The policy
question is not whether the system is conscious. It is what delegated powers
the system has and what obligations must attach to those powers.

### 5.4 Institutional Controls For Pillar A

Pillar A should now include a control checklist.

An institutional AI system should document its owner, model provider,
deployment environment, data sources, tool permissions, memory policy,
approval requirements, retention rules, logging design, audit process,
escalation path, incident owner, and exit plan.

The system should have its own identity rather than borrowing a human's
credentials. It should be scoped to specific tasks. It should not have blanket
access to email, files, payments, public posting, code deployment, resident
records, or customer data unless each access is justified and logged.

This is the institutional form of AH governance before AH law exists.

## Chapter 6: Pillar B Update - Value Capture In The Utility-Service Era

### 6.1 The Limits Of Compute Taxation

Volume 1 anticipated compute taxation as an early value-capture mechanism.
Compute and energy levies remain important because large model training and
frontier inference often require centralized infrastructure. Data centers are
visible, measurable, and energy-intensive. They provide a practical fiscal
base.

But compute taxation alone is insufficient. Smaller models, distillation,
quantization, local inference, caching, and specialized agents all reduce the
relationship between compute input and economic output. A small model running
locally may create large value with little marginal compute. A hosted agent may
capture value through workflow ownership, not raw tokens. A vendor may provide
free credits during adoption and collect value later through lock-in.

This is the efficiency paradox becoming real. The more efficient models
become, the less a simple input levy captures the value they produce.

### 6.2 Utility-Service Value Capture

AI value in the institutional phase is captured through utility-service
channels.

It appears in subscriptions, API usage, platform seats, cloud spend,
professional services, implementation contracts, training programs, model
credits, workflow automation fees, usage overages, app marketplaces, and
support contracts. It also appears in less visible forms: data gravity,
institutional memory, staff retraining around a platform, proprietary prompts,
non-exportable workflows, and switching costs.

Pillar B therefore needs to ask where autonomous cognitive work becomes
market power. The taxable or capturable base is not only compute. It is the
service relationship that monetizes AI-generated capacity.

For government and nonprofit institutions, procurement is the near-term
value-capture lever. Contracts can require public-benefit clauses, data
export, workflow handoff, local training, usage transparency, audit logs,
price caps, security controls, and reinvestment of savings into mission
capacity.

For states and nations, policy can combine compute levies, service taxes,
gross receipts taxes, market-access obligations, data-derived value rules,
public ownership stakes, or AI productivity funds.

### 6.3 Public Data And Community Value

Many AI systems improve through public, institutional, or community data. A
city's records, a nonprofit's client histories, a school's curriculum, a
hospital's workflows, a state's procurement data, or a community's language
patterns can become fuel for private model improvement.

Pillar B should treat data-derived value as part of the capture problem. If a
vendor uses public or community data to improve a system, then the public or
community should retain enforceable rights: deletion, opt-out, compensation,
shared access, public reporting, or reinvestment.

This is especially important for marginalized communities. AI adoption in
public-interest institutions should not convert vulnerable people's records
into private training assets without consent, benefit, and accountability.

### 6.4 Measurement

Institutions need a practical measurement model for AI-created value.

The first metric is avoided cost: hours saved, backlogs reduced, translation
capacity added, document processing accelerated, or error rates reduced.

The second metric is service improvement: faster response, more accessible
forms, better resident communication, broader language access, improved case
triage, or higher grant success.

The third metric is institutional capacity: staff trained, workflows
documented, local systems created, prompts exported, logs preserved, and
dependency reduced.

The fourth metric is public value retained: savings reinvested, data rights
preserved, infrastructure shared, or dividend-like benefits created.

Pillar B fails if it measures only vendor spend or staff reductions. It must
measure what value remains with the institution and the public.

## Chapter 7: Pillar C Update - Productivity Funds And Dividends

### 7.1 From Global Fund To Local Vessel

Volume 1 proposed a Global Productivity Fund. Volume 2 keeps that horizon but
adds local vessels. Before global coordination exists, institutions can create
smaller funds that preserve the same logic.

A municipality can create an AI Productivity Fund that receives a percentage
of verified savings from AI-assisted workflows and reinvests those funds into
resident services, staff training, open data infrastructure, cybersecurity, and
local digital capacity.

A nonprofit can create an AI Capacity Reserve that ensures grant-funded AI
projects leave behind staff training, documentation, local workflows, and
post-grant operating funds.

A state can create an Automation Dividend Pilot that uses AI-related service
taxes, procurement savings, data-center levies, or public equity returns to
fund direct benefits or public services.

An enterprise can create a workforce transition fund that shares AI
productivity gains with workers through training, wage support, shorter work
weeks, profit sharing, or internal mobility.

These are not substitutes for the GPF. They are prefigurative institutions.
They make the DSGM loop real at smaller scale.

### 7.2 Why Dividends Matter

The Automation Dividend is often misunderstood as welfare. It is better
understood as a claim on productive infrastructure.

The Alaska Permanent Fund shows the basic logic: a finite natural resource can
be converted into a long-term public asset that pays residents a dividend.
The analogy is imperfect because AI is not oil. But the institutional lesson is
useful. When a productive base creates extraordinary returns from a shared or
strategic resource, public ownership and dividends can convert extraction into
broad benefit.

AI draws on public science, public education, public infrastructure, labor
histories, language communities, user data, open-source software, and legal
systems. It is not created in a vacuum. A dividend recognizes that the
productive base is socially embedded.

The dividend also preserves demand. A fully automated economy still needs
buyers, communities, legitimacy, and social peace. If AI reduces labor income
without replacing purchasing power, productivity can coexist with instability.

The policy evidence should be read with humility. IMF fiscal-policy work, CBO
analysis, ILO job-exposure research, OECD labor-market studies, and World Bank
UBI guidance all point toward uncertainty rather than a single settled formula.
That uncertainty strengthens, rather than weakens, DSGM's institutional claim:
if AI productivity gains are uncertain in size but likely to be uneven in
distribution, then procurement, reinvestment, and dividend design should begin
while public institutions still have bargaining power.

### 7.3 Institutional Dividend Logic

At institutional scale, the dividend may not begin as cash. It may begin as
service capacity.

For a city, the dividend may be faster permitting, better translation,
accessible council records, lower administrative fees, or expanded public
hours.

For a nonprofit, it may be more clients served, more grants written, better
intake triage, or less staff burnout.

For a school district, it may be teacher support, multilingual family
communication, tutoring access, or administrative relief.

For a state, it may become direct payments, public broadband, cybersecurity
support, workforce retraining, or shared public AI infrastructure.

The key is that AI-created productivity must not vanish into vendor margin or
budget cuts alone. Some portion must return to the people and mission that made
the system legitimate.

### 7.4 Governance

Productivity funds require governance. Without insulation, funds become
ordinary budgets. Without democratic oversight, they become technocratic
slush funds. Without audit, they become slogans.

A DSGM-aligned productivity fund should have a public mandate, independent
oversight, transparent reporting, conflict-of-interest rules, annual audits,
clear reinvestment categories, and measurable public outcomes. It should
separate temporary pilot savings from durable revenue. It should avoid funding
core services with unstable speculative gains unless reserves exist.

The fund should also treat security as a cost of public benefit. AI savings
that ignore security are not real savings. They are deferred risk.

## Chapter 8: Private SLM-Powered Agency

### 8.1 Definition

Private SLM-powered agency is an institutional AI architecture built around
smaller models, local or private inference, retrieval over approved sources,
tool use through explicit permissions, human approval, logging, and selective
escalation to frontier models.

It is not merely running a small chatbot on a laptop. It is a governed system
for routine institutional cognition.

The components are:

- a task-appropriate model;
- a private or controlled execution environment;
- approved document retrieval;
- explicit tool schemas;
- least-privilege permissions;
- human review for high-risk actions;
- logs and evidence records;
- model routing rules;
- exportable prompts and workflows;
- incident response and rollback.

### 8.2 Why SLM Agency Preserves Institutionality

Institutions are not only collections of data. They are collections of
judgment, process, memory, duty, and legitimacy. If AI adoption moves those
elements into a vendor-controlled platform, the institution becomes dependent.

Private SLM agency preserves institutionality because it keeps routine
cognitive work near the institution. Staff can inspect workflows. Records can
remain local. Prompts can be exported. Logs can be audited. Models can be
swapped. Sensitive work can stay inside controlled environments. Frontier
systems can be called only when needed.

This matters for nonprofits and governments because they serve people who may
not have bargaining power. A resident seeking public benefits, a client seeking
housing help, a student needing translation, or a small business applying for
permits should not become part of a hidden vendor dependency chain without
public oversight.

### 8.3 Routing, Not Romanticism

Private SLM agency should not become ideology. Some tasks need frontier
systems. Hard reasoning, high-stakes legal analysis, advanced code repair,
complex scientific interpretation, and uncertain multi-domain planning may
require stronger models.

The correct pattern is routing. Routine tasks should use local or private
models where sufficient. Ambiguous or difficult tasks should escalate. High-risk
tasks should require human approval regardless of model. Sensitive tasks should
prefer local controls unless the hosted environment provides superior verified
security and governance.

Routing makes cost visible. It also makes value capture possible. If every
task goes to a frontier model, the institution cannot tell where value is being
created or whether cheaper local systems would suffice.

### 8.4 Relationship To Open Source

Open source and open weights matter because they reduce dependency, allow
inspection, support customization, and enable local deployment. But open does
not automatically mean safe. An open model can be poisoned, misused,
misconfigured, or embedded in an insecure pipeline. An open connector can
become a supply-chain risk. A local deployment can leak data if logs, files,
and credentials are mishandled.

The Volume 2 position is therefore pragmatic: prefer open and private where
capable, but require provenance, testing, sandboxing, monitoring, and human
governance.

## Chapter 9: Security And Resilience

### 9.1 Security As Agency

Security is not separate from the DSGM argument. It is a condition of agency.
An institution that cannot secure its AI system cannot govern it. An
institution that cannot audit its agent cannot hold it accountable. An
institution that cannot revoke access cannot exit.

AI security risks matter because agentic systems blur boundaries between
information and action. A document is not only a document if it can instruct an
agent. A retrieved webpage is not only context if it can manipulate tool use.
A model dependency is not only a file if it can influence decisions across the
institution.

### 9.2 Supply-Chain Attacks

AI supply chains include model weights, datasets, embeddings, retrieval
indexes, plugins, MCP servers, packages, containers, APIs, prompts, fine-tunes,
vector databases, evaluation harnesses, and logging systems. Each can become a
compromise point.

A malicious connector can exfiltrate data. A poisoned dataset can shape model
behavior. A tampered model file can produce unsafe outputs. A vulnerable
package can compromise the host. A prompt template can encode hidden behavior.
An unreviewed MCP server can become a privileged bridge into institutional
systems.

Institutions should require provenance for models and dependencies, dependency
inventory, version pinning, review of connectors and plugins, integrity checks
where feasible, and separation between experimental and production systems.

### 9.3 Prompt Injection And Context Attacks

Prompt injection is especially dangerous because agents consume untrusted
language. Emails, PDFs, webpages, tickets, transcripts, and records can carry
instructions that conflict with institutional intent.

The correct posture is risk reduction, not magical prevention. Institutions
should isolate untrusted content, limit tool permissions, require approval for
consequential actions, log retrieved sources, evaluate prompt-injection
scenarios, and avoid connecting agents to systems where a successful injection
would be catastrophic.

This matters for public institutions because they process adversarial content
by design. A city receives complaints, records, filings, and public comments. A
court receives motions. A school receives emails. A nonprofit receives client
documents. AI agents in these contexts must assume that some inputs are
hostile or manipulative.

### 9.4 Excessive Agency And Takeover

The term "AI takeover" can distract if it is treated only as a speculative
future event. For institutions, takeover begins as operational loss of control.

An agent with broad credentials, tool access, and memory can act beyond its
approved institutional scope. It can send messages, modify files, approve
requests, delete records, deploy code, route money, or reveal data. It does
not need consciousness to create takeover-like outcomes. It only needs
permissions and a failure mode.

This framing keeps the dissertation evidence-led. International AI safety
reporting treats catastrophic loss-of-control scenarios as an advanced-risk
area that requires continued research rather than a claim that present systems
already possess independent takeover capability. Institutional planning should
therefore translate the phrase into immediate control failures that leaders can
actually govern: excessive agency, compromised connectors, poisoned knowledge
bases, unbounded spending, unavailable logs, and weak recovery procedures.

The prevention is not philosophy. It is control design: least privilege,
segmented identities, approval gates, sandboxing, rate limits, action caps,
monitoring, rollback, and incident response.

### 9.5 Incident Response

AI incidents require specific playbooks. An institution should be able to
disable agent tools, revoke tokens, rotate credentials, preserve logs, freeze
workflows, export evidence, notify affected parties, restore clean state, and
review root causes.

The incident plan should cover data leakage, prompt injection, malicious tool
use, model-routing errors, unsafe generated code, policy violations, cost
spikes, unauthorized access, and vendor outages.

An AI adoption contract that lacks incident response is incomplete. It assumes
success and leaves the institution exposed when failure occurs.

## Chapter 10: First-Party Validation - Agency, NanoClaw, And OpenClaw

### 10.1 Why First-Party Evidence Matters

The companion `agency` repository is first-party validation of the harness
plateau. It is not used to prove global adoption. It is used to show that the
dissertation's technical claims map to real implementation concerns.

The repository describes a Rust runtime substrate moving older Agency harness
ideas into narrower NanoClaw contracts. It includes OpenClaw gateway execution,
provider routing, execution evidence, tool adapter contracts, runtime
inspection, state residues, health checks, safety gates, command safety,
output safety, and model routing.

Those elements match Volume 2's claim that institutional AI is not only a
model problem. It is a runtime problem.

### 10.2 Execution Evidence

Execution evidence matters because agent work must be inspectable. If an agent
claims a task is complete, the institution needs to know what workspace it
used, what commands ran, what model was called, what artifacts were produced,
what verification occurred, what blockers appeared, and what provenance IDs
exist.

This maps directly to Pillar A. Accountable operational agency requires
evidence. Without evidence, the owner cannot govern the system. Without
governance, AH-style obligations are impossible.

### 10.3 Provider Routing

Provider routing matters because models differ in cost, capability, privacy,
jurisdiction, and risk. A runtime that can route deterministic tasks away from
paid inference, routine tasks to smaller models, difficult tasks to stronger
models, and high-risk tasks to special review creates the practical foundation
for Pillar B.

If routing is hidden, value capture is hidden. If routing is visible,
institutions can measure spend, escalation, fallback, and productivity.

### 10.4 Tool Contracts

Tool contracts matter because tools are where language becomes action. A model
that can only draft text is one kind of risk. A model that can call shell,
browser, email, database, payment, or deployment tools is another.

The `agency` work validates the need for explicit request-plane policy,
capability requirements, approval policy, required artifacts, failure
blockers, workspace expectations, and human oversight. Those are the same
controls Volume 2 recommends for institutions.

## Chapter 11: Implementation Pathways

### 11.1 Nonprofits

Nonprofits should treat AI fellowships, grants, credits, and training programs
as capacity-building opportunities only if the capacity remains after the
program ends.

Minimum requirements should include documented workflows, staff training,
exportable prompts, local copies of records, clear data-use terms, security
review, and a post-grant operating plan. If a fellowship creates workflows that
only the fellow or vendor can operate, the nonprofit has not built capacity.

A nonprofit AI Capacity Reserve can receive a portion of grant funding or
verified savings to maintain workflows, train staff, and preserve independence.

### 11.2 Municipalities

Municipalities should focus on resident-facing value and public accountability.
AI can help with translation, records search, permit triage, council
summaries, service routing, and document accessibility. But public offices
must avoid opaque decision systems and non-portable vendor memory.

A municipal AI Productivity Fund can receive a percentage of verified
AI-generated savings and reinvest in public service, cybersecurity, local
digital infrastructure, staff training, and open civic tooling.

Procurement should require data export, audit logs, public reporting,
accessibility, security controls, human appeal, and clear ownership of
AI-assisted decisions.

### 11.3 SMEs

Small and midsize businesses are likely to adopt AI through bundled software,
automation services, and inexpensive agents. The risk is dependence on
platforms they cannot negotiate with.

SMEs should prioritize portable workflows, local copies of customer and
financial data, vendor exit paths, model-cost transparency, and private SLM
options for routine back-office work. They should avoid granting AI systems
broad access to payments, customer communications, or financial records without
approval gates.

### 11.4 Enterprises

Enterprises have more resources but larger attack surfaces. They should build
model-routing governance, AI asset inventories, tool permission registries,
red-team programs, security monitoring, and workforce transition funds.

Enterprise AI productivity should be shared through training, internal
mobility, worker participation, shorter feedback cycles, and clear rules about
automation-driven displacement.

### 11.5 State And Federal Government

State and federal agencies should treat AI adoption as public infrastructure.
They need procurement standards, model and tool inventories, cloud and local
deployment options, security baselines, incident reporting, public value
metrics, and cross-agency shared services.

Government should also study AI productivity funds, data-center levies,
service taxes, public equity models, and dividend pilots. Waiting for full AGI
before building public value vessels would repeat the mistake Volume 1 warned
against.

## Chapter 12: Policy Recommendations

### 12.1 For Institutions

Institutions should adopt AI only with documented ownership, data rights,
workflow portability, model routing, tool permissions, security review,
logging, incident response, and exit plans.

They should classify tasks by model tier: deterministic logic first, private
SLMs for routine bounded work, frontier models for hard reasoning or
specialist escalation, and human approval for high-risk actions.

They should measure AI value by capacity retained, not only speed or cost
reduction.

### 12.2 For Policymakers

Policymakers should update procurement law for agentic AI. Requirements should
cover data export, logs, model routing, public value, security, human appeal,
and post-contract portability.

They should study compute levies, AI service taxes, market-access rules,
public-benefit contract clauses, data-derived value obligations, and AI
productivity funds.

They should create public SLM infrastructure for common civic and nonprofit
tasks so every organization does not have to negotiate separately with
frontier vendors.

### 12.3 For Vendors

Vendors should treat institutional agency as a product requirement. They
should provide exportable workflows, clear data-use terms, transparent model
routing, security documentation, incident support, and non-extractive pricing
after grants or pilots end.

Vendors that serve public-interest institutions should not frame adoption
success only as seats, credits, usage, or dependency. Success should include
staff capacity, public value, portability, and safe operation.

### 12.4 For Open-Source Builders

Open-source builders should focus on the missing middle: reliable SLM agency
for bounded institutional workflows. The most valuable tools will not merely
run models locally. They will provide retrieval, permissions, logs, tool
contracts, sandboxing, model routing, red-team tests, and exportable workflows.

Open-source communities should also take supply-chain security seriously.
Trustworthy local AI requires signed releases, dependency hygiene,
provenance, vulnerability reporting, and secure defaults.

## Chapter 13: Ethical Considerations

### 13.1 Human Agency

AI adoption should increase human agency, not replace it with platform
dependency. Staff should understand workflows, review outputs, and retain the
ability to operate without the vendor. Residents, clients, customers, and
workers should have appeal paths when AI affects them.

### 13.2 Equity

Public-interest AI must avoid turning vulnerable communities into testing
grounds. Data use should be limited, consent-aware, and publicly accountable.
Benefits should flow back to the communities whose data, labor, language, and
needs shape the system.

### 13.3 Labor

Institutions should avoid using AI productivity only to cut workers. The DSGM
position is not anti-productivity. It is anti-extraction. Productivity gains
should support training, transition, reduced administrative burden, better
services, and shared prosperity.

### 13.4 Security And Care

Security is an ethical duty. A public agency that leaks resident data through
an AI workflow has harmed people. A nonprofit that feeds sensitive client data
into an insecure system has breached trust. An enterprise that deploys
over-permissioned agents without controls has created preventable risk.

## Chapter 14: Limitations And Future Research

This dissertation is a framework update, not a final statutory or econometric
model. Future work should include fiscal simulations for AI service taxes and
productivity funds; legal drafting for AH accountability; procurement clause
templates; municipal pilots; nonprofit case studies; SLM benchmark suites for
institutional tasks; security red-team methods for agent workflows; and
governance models for public AI infrastructure.

The most important empirical question is which workflows can be reliably
served by private SLM agency today. The answer will vary by language, sector,
risk level, document quality, retrieval design, and staff capacity.

The most important policy question is how to capture AI-generated value before
institutional dependency becomes too strong to bargain against.

## Conclusion

Volume 1 argued that AI would force a redesign of the social contract. Volume
2 argues that the redesign has already begun inside institutions.

The AI utility layer is forming through grants, fellowships, government
products, enterprise copilots, agent frameworks, model APIs, local inference,
and security-sensitive supply chains. This supports the DSGM thesis. AI is
becoming a productive actor whose output must be governed, secured, taxed, and
reinvested into public benefit.

The first visible form of Artificial Humanity is operational agency. The first
visible form of value capture is procurement and workflow ownership. The first
visible form of the Global Productivity Fund is the institutional productivity
fund. The first visible form of the Automation Dividend may be public capacity
retained from AI-generated gains.

Institutions face a choice. They can become tenants inside someone else's
intelligence infrastructure, or they can build and govern their own agency.
Private SLM-powered agency is not the whole answer, but it is the steering
direction that keeps the choice open.

The goal is not smaller AI for its own sake. The goal is accountable,
portable, secure, public-serving intelligence.

## Appendix A: Institutional AI Maturity Model

### Level 0: Unmanaged Use

Staff use public AI tools informally. There is no inventory, data policy,
approval workflow, logging, or exit plan.

### Level 1: Policy Awareness

The institution has acceptable-use guidance, basic training, and a list of
approved tools. Sensitive data restrictions exist, but workflows remain
informal.

### Level 2: Managed Pilots

AI pilots have owners, documented use cases, data boundaries, tool
permissions, human review, and evaluation metrics.

### Level 3: Audited Agency

Agents have scoped identities, least-privilege tools, logs, model routing,
retrieval controls, security testing, and exportable workflows.

### Level 4: Private SLM Agency

Routine workflows run through private or local models where feasible.
Frontier models are used through routing policies. Staff can inspect and
operate the system.

### Level 5: DSGM-Aligned Institution

The institution measures AI-generated productivity, reinvests gains into
public or mission benefit, contributes to shared infrastructure, and maintains
security, portability, and accountability as permanent governance functions.

## Appendix B: Minimum Contract Clauses

An institutional AI contract should include:

- data-use limits and training opt-out;
- export rights for data, prompts, logs, workflows, embeddings, and agent
  configuration;
- model and provider disclosure;
- tool permission registry;
- human approval requirements;
- security and supply-chain documentation;
- incident response obligations;
- post-pilot pricing;
- deletion and retention terms;
- public-benefit or reinvestment obligations where public funds are involved;
- staff training and handoff requirements;
- audit rights;
- termination and transition support.

## Appendix C: AI Security Control Baseline

The minimum control baseline should include:

- AI asset inventory;
- model and dependency provenance;
- reviewed connectors and MCP servers;
- least-privilege agent identities;
- sandboxed execution;
- prompt-injection testing;
- retrieval-source integrity checks;
- logging of prompts, sources, tool calls, approvals, model routing, artifacts,
  and failures;
- red-team testing for data leakage and excessive agency;
- incident response playbooks;
- credential revocation and rotation;
- cost anomaly monitoring;
- recovery and rollback procedures.
